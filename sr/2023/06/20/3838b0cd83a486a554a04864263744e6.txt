iUwe Seeler (German pronunciation: [ˈuːvə ˈzeːlɐ]; 5 November 1936 – 21 July
i2022) was a German footballer and football official. As a striker, he was a
iprolific scorer for Hamburger SV and also made 72 appearances for the West
iGermany national team. Widely regarded as one of the greatest players in
iGerman football history,[2] Seeler was named one of FIFA's 100 greatest
iliving
iplayers by Pelé in 2004. He was the first football player to be awarded the
iCommander's Cross of the Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany.
i
i===== Playing career =====
i
iSeeler followed in his father's footsteps as a player for Hamburger SV,
imaking his first team debut in 1954 in a DFB-Pokal match, aged just under 18,
iscoring four goals (8–2 vs. Holstein Kiel). In later years, despite tempting
ioffers from Italian and Spanish clubs, he remained loyal to Hamburg, working
ion a second career as a merchant besides playing football.
i
iSeeler was a gifted, powerful, and prolific striker who, among other things,
iwas most of all renowned for his leadership, consistency, overhead kicks, and
iaerial ability.[3] He scored 137 times in 239 Bundesliga games, 43 times in
i72
iinternational games for the German national team, and 21 times in 29 European
iclub tournament games. He was captain of both his club team and the national
iteam for many years. He and his club won the German championship in 1960 and
ithe DFB-Pokal in 1963. He was top scorer of the first Bundesliga season in
i1963–64 and German Footballer of the Year in 1960, 1964, and 1970. During the
i1960–61 season, Seeler, alongside his brother Dieter [de], helped to lead
iHamburger SV to the semi-finals of the European Cup, where they narrowly
imissed out on the final against Benfica, losing out to Barcelona in a
iplay-off
imatch. During the 1967–68 season, Seeler also helped Hamburg to reach the
ifinal of the European Cup Winners' Cup, finishing the competition as top
iscorer, only to lose out to AC Milan.[4]
i
iIn 1978, he and his former teammate Franz-Josef Hönig played for Cork Celtic
iF.C. in a one-off sponsored event. Seeler had ended his active playing career
iin 1972.[5] However, this match turned out to be an official League of
iIreland
ione and Uwe scored twice.[6] Thus, his overall record of goals scored in
ileague and championship matches adds up to 446 (Hamburger SV 444,[7] Cork
iCeltic 2). His 404 goals in German Oberliga and Bundesliga league games is a
irecord that stands as of today, his 406 goals in league games overall making
ihim the second-best German goalscorer behind Gerd Müller.[8]
i
iSeeler participated in the same four FIFA World Cups as Pelé did: 1958, 1962,
i1966, and 1970. Of those West German World Cup teams, the 1966 side reached
ithe final, where they lost to host nation England 4–2 in extra time.[9] In
i1958, the German team finished in fourth place; in 1970 the West German team
ifinished in third after being eliminated by European rivals Italy in the
isemi-finals, following a closely fought 4–3 extra-time loss, a match often
ireferred to as the "Game of the Century".[4] In the previous round, a
ibackward
isecond-half header against England tied the score 2–2, a game West Germany
iwent on to win 3–2.[10]
i
iAlthough Seeler never won a World Cup (his involvement as a player in the
itournament started four years after West Germany won their first World Cup
i(1954) and ended four years before they won their second (1974)), he had a
iprolific career in the tournament; he was the first player ever to appear in
i20 World Cup matches (he retired with 21 matches played, tied for third
iall-time); the first ever to score in four World Cups (beating Pelé by only a
ifew minutes), and the first player to score at least two goals in each of
ifour
iWorld Cups (matched in 2014 by his compatriot Miroslav Klose). He also ranks
ithird in all-time minutes played in World Cups, with 1,980, behind Paolo
iMaldini and Lothar Matthäus. In total, he scored nine goals across the four
iWorld Cups in which he played, as well as three goals in World Cup Qualifying
imatches; he scored 43 times in 72 international appearances between 1954 and
i1970.[11] A photo of a dejected Seeler leaving the field having just been
idefeated in the 1966 World Cup Final was voted as Photo of the Century by
ikicker magazine.[12]
i
i===== Career after football =====
i
iHe had a two-and-a-half-year tenure as president of Hamburger SV, which began
iin 1995, and ended in resignation in 1998 due to a financial scandal, for
iwhich he took responsibility.[13] Seeler, however, was not himself implicated
iin the irregularities.[14]
i
i===== Recognition =====
i
iSeeler was a tremendously popular player due to his fairness and modesty and
iis still widely called Uns Uwe (West Low German: Our Uwe) in Hamburg and the
isurrounding area. The DFB (German FA) made him the second honorary captain of
ithe German national team in 1972 (the first being Fritz Walter). In 2003, he
ibecame an honorary citizen of his hometown Hamburg; the first time the honor
iwas bestowed on a sportsman.[15] That year he also published his memoirs
iDanke, Fußball ("Thank you, football"). 2005 saw the unveiling of a giant
imonument in front of the Hamburger SV stadium depicting his right
ifoot.[14][16]
i
i===== Film appearances =====
i
iSeeler appeared in a cameo role in the popular 1972 Heinz Erhardt comedy
iWilli wird das Kind schon schaukeln (English title: Willi Manages the Whole
iThing), playing himself.[17]
i
i===== Personal life =====
i
iFrom 1959 until his death, Uwe Seeler lived with his wife in Harksheide,
itoday a district of Norderstedt in the Hamburg Metropolitan Region.[18]
i
iSeeler's grandson, Levin Öztunalı, is also a professional footballer.[19]
i
i===== Death =====
i
iSeeler died on 21 July 2022, aged 85, in his home in Norderstedt.[20] He was
ihonoured later in the day with a moment of silence before the UEFA Women's
iEuro 2022 quarter-final between Germany and Austria.[21] The following second
imatchday of the 2. Bundesliga, where Hamburg played a home match against
iHansa
iRostock, as well as the first round of the DFB-Pokal also began with a minute
iof silence in Seeler's tribute. The Bundesliga home game of HSV featured
isupporters dressed in black displaying a banner reading "loyal and modest –
ithe greatest of all time" ("Loyal und bescheiden - der Größte aller Zeiten)
iin
ihonor of Seeler's club career.
